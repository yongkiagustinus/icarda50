The Decade At A Glance

A Vision Manifested

1970s

ICARDA was born out of CGIAR’s recognition that vast, semi-arid regions were underserved by science. From identifying the need (1972) to signing the charter (1975) and breaking ground in Tel Hadya, near Aleppo, Syria (1977), ICARDA’s first years were about turning a vision into an institution grounded in regional relevance, systems thinking, and farmer partnership.

Science for the Drylands

1980s

During the 1980s, ICARDA established new offices across North Africa and West Asia, as well as its first genebank in Tel Hadya, Syria. ICARDA began producing tangible impacts through early farmer-centered research models, including the development of digital data systems and the introduction of the first crop varieties.

Expanding the Horizon

1990s

The 1990s were a decade of growth and connection in which ICARDA expanded its partnerships into new geographies, from Central Asia to the Arabian Peninsula, while strengthening national research systems. Its genetic resources collection surpassed 90,000 plant accessions, and participatory, cross-border research flourished. ICARDA’s leadership in water-use efficiency and rangeland management cemented its global relevance in these areas.

Adapting to a Changing World

2000s

Conflict, drought, and shifting global landscape and priorities tested ICARDA’s resilience. But, from helping rebuild Afghanistan’s agriculture to streamlining its research into six major programs, ICARDA became more agile and focused. The decade ended with a harsh drought in the region, underscoring the importance of climate adaptation and reaffirming ICARDA’s role as a partner in resilience and recovery.

Resilience Through Decentralization

2010s

The conflict that erupted in Syria in 2011 led to ICARDA reinventing itself as a decentralized network of research hubs across Lebanon, Morocco, Ethiopia, Egypt, Jordan, and Türkiye. The move reflected ICARDA’s founding belief that science should adapt to the place and that every dryland ecosystem, no matter how harsh, demands its own solutions. This transformation fueled its global dryland research programs and a landmark regional food security project. During this period, ICARDA also withdrew some of its seed collections from the Svalbard Global Seed Vault, becoming the first organization to do so, to regenerate and duplicate them, before safely redepositing them to ensure the long-term preservation of vital genetic resources.

The Age of Acceleration

2020s

Despite a global pandemic, research continued in over 60 countries. ICARDA inaugurated a next-generation genebank, launched AI-driven breeding tools, updated its research strategy, and co-led the Global Strategy for Resilient Drylands with CGIAR partners. With new initiatives addressing biosecurity and establishing the African Plant Breeding Accelerator, ICARDA entered its 50th year as a global leader shaping the future of drylands.

The Way Forward

ICARDA was created to serve regions overlooked by conventional models, yet central to the future of food. As climate volatility intensifies and resources dwindle, dryland agriculture is no longer a niche concern; it is a global one.

The next chapter will demand faster science, stronger partnerships, and deeper local engagement. From predictive breeding to water-smart farming, from genebanks to farmer networks, ICARDA’s work will continue to bridge science and survival.

Fifty years on, the mandate endures: in partnership with key governments and global actors, we confront the unique challenges facing the drylands to secure food, livelihoods, and ecosystems for the region and beyond.

A Vision Manifested

1972 Researching the region.

Identifying the Gap

A CGIAR technical mission concluded that the semi-arid winter rainfall zone, which covers much of the Near East and North Africa, is a critical ecological area with distinct crops and pressing agricultural problems. Despite its significance, the region was underserved by existing agricultural research centers, making the case for a new center with its own science, dedicated to drylands.

1975 Events in the Evolution of ICARDA.

Establishing the Charter 

ICARDA’s founding charter was signed with a clear mandate to raise and stabilize food production across the drylands. Its initial focus was on key regional crops such as barley, lentil, and faba bean, while also supporting wheat, chickpea, and other crops’ research, in partnership with established sister CGIAR centers. Beyond crops, the charter called for advancing farming and livestock systems, strengthening regional and global collaboration, and training a new generation of dryland agriculture scientists.  

1977 A view of ICARDA’s facilities in Tal Hadya, Syria, 2007.

Headquarters Near Aleppo

Possible locations in Lebanon, Syria, and Iran were explored due to their ecological diversity. The President of Syria offered land near Aleppo, where ICARDA ultimately established its headquarters, in Tel Hadya. Research began on drought-tolerant crops, rainwater management, and mixed farming systems, efforts that would later define ICARDA’s global leadership in dryland agriculture. 

1978–79 ICARDA Activities.

Farming Systems Take Center Stage

Departing from the traditional top-down model of “technology transfer,” ICARDA prioritized research on farming systems. On-farm trials, crop-livestock integration, and participatory methods became key to its approach, a departure that many others would later adopt.

Science for the Drylands

1983 Introduction of Computing Technology at ICARDA.

Early Digital Transitions and Preservation of Genetic Commons

ICARDA began laying the groundwork for research computing by recruiting biometricians, developing custom software, and expanding computer services. These investments supported crop modeling, varietal trials, and training, long before digital tools were commonplace in the region. A Genetic Resources Unit was established to conserve seed diversity for dryland crops. Over time, it became one of the most comprehensive collections of its kind, serving as a valuable resource for crop breeders and scientists worldwide.

1984 Nile Valley Project demonstrations in Egypt.

Expanding Partnerships and Impact

The Nile Valley Project achieved strong results in Egypt and Sudan, leading Ethiopia to join the partnership to boost faba bean productivity and pest resistance. In Tunisia, the Crop/Livestock Systems projects advanced through national collaborations. ICARDA also expanded its footprint in Pakistan as a key implementing institution for multiple research initiatives. Extensive germplasm collection continued, with more than 5,000 barley entries documented, while training and capacity development accelerated, supported by a growing body of publications in Arabic.

1985 ICARDA's crop varieties and research areas.

New Crop Varieties

Just a decade after its founding, ICARDA’s early breeding work began to bear fruit with the release of three barley varieties in Tunisia, two durum wheat varieties in Algeria, a lentil variety in Ethiopia, and a chickpea variety in Cyprus. These initial releases demonstrated that a center established in the drylands could have a significant impact across its unique yet diverse ecosystems.  

1989 ICARDA's controlled environment becomes fully operational at its main station at Tel Hadya.

Expanding Research Horizons 

The Mashreq Project was launched in Jordan, Iraq, Syria, and Lebanon to tackle rangeland degradation, forage shortages, and low livestock productivity by integrating crops and livestock through multidisciplinary research. Experimentation moved out of research stations and into farmers’ fields in Syria, Morocco, Tunisia, and Sudan, utilizing participatory approaches to adapt technologies for barley, lentils, wheat, and faba beans in real-world, low-input systems. That same year, ICARDA’s controlled environment facility became fully operational at its main station in Tel Hadya, providing advanced infrastructure for plant research and disease screening.

Broadening the Genetic Base and Regional Reach

ICARDA’s plant genetic resources grew to over 91,000 accessions. Major missions collected stress-tolerant barley in Tibet and pasture legumes across Morocco. Over 78,000 accessions were transferred to upgraded storage, with 6,132 of them preserved long-term at –22°C. ICARDA initiated systematic duplication and global distribution, launching ecogeographical databases that cover more than 50,000 accessions of wild wheat and pasture legumes. Regionally, ICARDA expanded its reach with the launch of the Highland and Arabian Peninsula Regional Programs, strengthening collaboration across drylands from North Africa to Central Asia.

1995 Syrian Farmers discuss changes in land use during the past decade with ICARDA scientist.

Expanding the Network 

ICARDA hosted the first regional forum of national research systems in West Asia and North Africa, strengthening ties between national partners and CGIAR. Yemen and Eritrea joined the Nile Valley Regional Program, which was subsequently renamed the Nile Valley and Red Sea Regional Program. New agreements were signed with agricultural academies in Central Asia to collaborate on research and the conservation of genetic resources. That year also saw ICARDA’s designation as the CGIAR lead center for soil water-use efficiency, on-farm water husbandry, and the use of shrubs for animal forage.

Adapting to a Changing World

2001 ICARDA in Afghanistan.

Rebuilding Afghanistan’s Agriculture 

After years of war, ICARDA led a recovery effort in Afghanistan through the Future Harvest Consortium along with sister CGIAR centers. Tens of thousands of farming households received seed, while research stations and training programs were revived. It was the organization’s first large-scale post-conflict initiative.

2004 Training farmers in improved techniques of processing of milk derivatives.

Redesigning Research for Greater Impact 

ICARDA has redesigned its research portfolio, consolidating 19 projects into six Mega Projects that focus on human welfare, agricultural productivity, economic growth, and environmental protection in dry areas. The new structure, aligned with CGIAR priorities and the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), laid the foundation for a more integrated and impact-driven research agenda, which took effect in January 2005.

2008 Testing lentils for heat tolerance at Tel Hadya.

A Drought, and a Test 

The launch of ICARDA’s 2007–2016 Strategic Plan coincided with the region’s worst drought in nearly four decades – only 210 mm of rainfall in Aleppo, compared to the average of 340 mm, and crop yields across West Asia and North Africa were slashed by half. Yet ICARDA-developed cereals and legumes, bred for resilience, performed strongly. The crisis, exacerbated by the global food price shock, highlighted the vulnerability of dryland countries and reinforced ICARDA’s mission to advance climate adaptation, food security, and rural resilience. Governments and international bodies have turned to ICARDA for solutions, validating its role as a trusted partner in crisis management.

Resilience Through Decentralization

2010 ICARDA DG Dr. Mahmoud Solh meeting with India President.

Leading Innovation Across Drylands 

ICARDA was designated Lead Center for the CGIAR Research Program on Dryland Systems, while also contributing to programs on wheat, legumes, climate, livestock, water, and policy. ICARDA also increasingly leveraged biotechnology, geographic information systems (GIS), simulation modeling, and market analysis to accelerate adoption and impact, launching new cross-disciplinary research and development (R&D) initiatives. ICARDA also hosted visits from policymakers across the region, including the President of India, leading to new agreements to scale innovations across South Asia and beyond.

2011 EFSAC Project.

From Displacement to Regional Hubs 

The conflict in Syria compelled ICARDA to relocate a significant portion of its research activities. Instead of consolidating elsewhere, it decentralized, establishing research hubs in Lebanon, Morocco, Ethiopia, Egypt, Jordan, and Türkiye. CGIAR had already anticipated the need for multiple research stations across different ecologies in the drylands, all under ICARDA, making this decentralized approach part of ICARDA’s DNA from the start. That same year, ICARDA launched the Enhancing Food Security in Arab Countries project. Over 12 years, across 10 countries, and with the support of five donors, it evolved into a flagship model of regional cooperation, enhancing wheat-based food security through shared science, capacity building, and policy support. The project ultimately won the Almarai Prize in 2025. 

2015-16 Margret Thalwitz ICARDA BC - Aly Abousabaa ICARDA DG - Ahmed Amri, ICARDA’s Head of Genetic Resources - Marie Haga, Executive Director of the Crop Trust at Svalbard in 2017.

A First from Svalbard 

In a historic first, following the loss of access to its Tel Hadya genebank during the Syrian conflict, ICARDA withdrew seeds from the Svaldbard Global Seed Vault. ICARDA’s collection, one of the world’s most diverse dryland seed banks, was then safely regenerated and secured in the newly established genebanks in Lebanon and Morocco, with a total of 46,000 seed accessions in trust. ICARDA later redeposited 15,420 seed samples to the Svalbard Global Seed Vault, securing them once again for long-term global conservation. Beyond conservation, ICARDA expanded its impact by introducing heat-tolerant wheat across hot African countries, including Sudan, Nigeria, Senegal, and Mauritania, where wheat was previously considered a minor crop. In Ethiopia, ICARDA’s improved legume varieties turned around the country’s declining trend in food legume production, where the higher yields encouraged farmers to plant more for domestic consumption as well as exports to Sub-Saharan Africa and the Middle East.

2017 Farmer at ICARDA's Terbol Station, Lebanon Photo Roni Ziade.

Planning for a Warmer Future 

A new ten-year strategy placed climate resilience, sustainable food systems, and regional policy engagement at the core of ICARDA’s research. The plan aimed to meet growing environmental pressures with applied, regionally relevant science.

2018 Tunisian Woman Farmer holding the handheld seeder CLCA Project.

Integrating Crop‑Livestock with Conservation Agriculture 

In addition to returning its seed samples to the Svalbard Global Seed Vault for long-term conservation, Guided by decades of crop-livestock systems research, ICARDA launched an IFAD-funded regional initiative to combine conservation agriculture with livestock production. Piloted in Tunisia and Algeria and expanded through CIMMYT to Bolivia and Mexico, the project helped around 2,200 farmers, 40% of whom were women, adopt no‑till, cover cropping, and forage systems. It boosted grain yields, cut costs, reduced water usage, and paved the way for a new path in livestock-crop integration in drylands. 

The Age of Acceleration

Research Continues, Remotely

Amid the COVID-19 pandemic, ICARDA sustained operations in over 60 countries. From digital agronomy to smallholder seed systems, research and extension are being adapted for remote delivery, reinforcing the institution’s decentralized approach.

2022 Aly Abousabaa ICARDA DG and Former MoA (Morocco) Mohamed Sadiki Cutting the Genebank Ribbon Photo James Pursey.

Infrastructure and Innovation 

ICARDA officially inaugurated a state-of-the-art genebank in Morocco. ICARDA's genbanks in Lebanon and Morocco, embedded within the overall CGIAR genebank system, work in tandem to collect essential plant specimens from the region and beyond. Both genebanks work with crop breeders to evaluate and disseminate adapted crop varieties to farmers, global researchers, and breeding programs. ICARDA's seeds are International Public Goods accessible to crop breeders and researchers worldwide. To date, around 85,000 accessions are available to global breeding programs and researchers, with around 20,000 seed samples distributed annually. Also in 2022, ICARDA launched its Integrated Desert Farming Innovation Platform. The program is designed to test the limits of viable agriculture in desert areas by transforming them into self-sustaining ecosystems through the combination of traditional oasis farming wisdom with modern innovations in water, energy, crop, and livestock management. 

2023 New Strategy.

A New Strategic Horizon 

A 2030 Research and Innovation Strategy was launched, focused on climate science, data-driven breeding, and inclusion. The plan positioned ICARDA to respond more quickly to environmental pressures and the shifting agricultural priorities in drylands.

2024 GSRD Launch at COP16 in KSA.

A Global Strategy for Dryland Resilience 

ICARDA and ICRISAT, together with 12 CGIAR sister centers, launched the Global Strategy for Resilient Drylands. The initiative aims to support over 2.7 billion people living in dry regions by scaling climate-smart crops, rangeland management, and sustainable water use. Rooted in decades of research, the strategy reflects a shift toward coordinated, system-level responses to climate and resource pressures. The year also marked new scientific frontiers as ICARDA researchers applied AI for genome-wide prediction, unlocking the vast genetic diversity held in genebanks and accelerating the breeding of crops tailored to withstand heat, drought, and disease. Meanwhile, ICARDA’s pioneering Community-Based Breeding Program, first introduced in Ethiopia in 2009, expanded to Mali in collaboration with ILRI, linking genetic improvement to enhanced animal health and improved feeding practices.

2025 2025 - RPW.

Accelerating Solutions

In partnership with the International Affairs Office at the United Arab Emirates (UAE) Presidential Court and the Gates Foundation, ICARDA initiated a large-scale regional project targeting the red palm weevil, a destructive pest that threatens date palm cultivation in the Arab region. The Consortium for Red Palm Weevil Control (C4RPWC) reflects a growing emphasis on biosecurity and rapid-response research. This year also saw the inauguration of the African Breeding Accelerator in Morocco, an advanced research and training hub equipped with cutting-edge tools to fast-track the development of climate-resilient wheat and barley. Designed to serve the entire continent, ABA will empower African scientists with next-generation breeding technologies, ensuring faster delivery of improved varieties to farmers facing intensifying climate pressures.
